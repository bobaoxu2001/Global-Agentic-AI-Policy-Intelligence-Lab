> Independent, AI-assisted analysis. Not legal advice, a company position, or evidence of internal delivery.

## Bottom line

This limited sample supports two recurring control priorities for action-taking agents: **disclose AI interaction and preserve accountable control over consequential actions**. The near-term recommendation is to build reusable disclosure, scoped authority, approval, logging, revocation, and redress capabilities while qualified reviewers determine which duties apply to each product-market configuration. The sample does not establish a universal legal floor or authorize launch.

## Source-backed position

**European Union.** Article 50 transparency obligations apply from **2026-08-02**; the Council-approved 2026 omnibus moves specified high-risk dates later [S01, S02]. GDPR Article 22 already addresses covered decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal or similarly significant effects [G8-EU-GDPR22]. **Inference:** the architecture should make AI interaction visible and keep a real, empowered human decision point where a consequential workflow might otherwise be solely automated. Whether review is legally “meaningful” remains fact-specific.

**United States.** The scoped preview does not identify a comprehensive federal AI-specific statute for private agent operators; that is not a conclusion that no law applies. Federal posture, state ADMT rules, sectoral rules, consumer protection, privacy, civil rights, and export controls require separate screens. BIS’s May/June 2026 materials expressly make headquarters and ultimate-parent facts relevant to specified advanced-computing licensing questions [S04, S05], while its January 2026 policy sets conditional case-by-case treatment for specified chip applications [S06]. **Inference:** product and cloud workflows need early ownership, end-use, state, sector, and customer screening.

**China.** The manifest-scoped preview includes binding generative-AI service and synthetic-content-labeling instruments [G8-CN-LABEL, G8-CN-GENAI]. **Inference:** disclosure cannot live only in terms of service; covered UI surfaces and file/output pipelines need coordinated visible and technical marking, with scope and exceptions validated against authoritative Chinese text.

**Singapore.** IMDA’s 2026 agentic-AI framework emphasizes bounding agent powers, meaningful human accountability, lifecycle controls, and end-user responsibility; its sandbox publication adds implementation observations [S08, S09]. These are governance signals, not legal authorization. **Inference:** they can organize control evidence and a low-risk assurance pilot while applicable data, consumer, sectoral, and other law is reviewed separately.

## Recommended position

1. **Disclosure by design.** Decide at the first interaction and output boundary what people must see and what machines must be able to detect.
2. **Authority by design.** Give agents explicit, expiring, revocable scopes; deny unknown tools by default.
3. **Oversight at the action boundary.** Put approval before high-impact or irreversible execution, with a trained reviewer who has time, information, and authority to change the outcome.
4. **Evidence at the runtime boundary.** Record principal, agent identity, delegated authority, tool, action, approval, time, and outcome outside model self-report.
5. **Market decisions after evidence.** Require current sources, product facts, tested controls, sensitivity, unresolved questions, and an accountable reviewer for each market configuration.

## Trade-offs

This baseline can slow high-impact actions, add data-governance obligations to logs, and require product-specific UI. Those costs should be compared with the option value of reusable controls: faster review, easier incident reconstruction, stronger enterprise assurance, and safer participation in emerging standards. Low-risk retrieval should not carry the same burden as payments, production changes, or consequential decisions.
